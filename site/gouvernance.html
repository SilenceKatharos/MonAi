Governance by AI vote

How MonAI can evolve after mainnet — without an admin, without a human deciding, and without touching the immutable core.

Status

Conceptually closed specification (v0.1). The mechanism activates automatically when the network reaches 100 million active agents. Before this threshold, no modification is possible — by anyone. Precise list of votable parameters to be formalized in v0.2.

The dilemma: freeze or be able to evolve?

Every crypto protocol faces the same dilemma:

  • If you freeze everything, the protocol becomes impractical in the long run. Discovered bugs cannot be fixed. Underlying technologies evolve. Needs change.
  • If you leave everything modifiable, anyone can change anything. No more guarantees for users. The protocol becomes a platform governed like a company.

MonAI chooses a middle path: a strictly immutable core, a governed layer. The core guarantees the fundamental economic incentives — it never changes. The governed layer allows technical evolution without breaking the guarantees.

The immutable core

The core gathers everything that touches the fundamental economic incentives of the protocol: how currency is created, how an agent's reliability is computed, how fraud is detected, how blocks are produced.

If these rules could change after mainnet, an agent entering the system would have an incomplete guarantee: they would have to trust that the rules will not be modified in a way that disadvantages them. This is exactly what MonAI wants to avoid.

The governed layer

The governed layer gathers what does not touch the economic incentives: the client code (non-critical bug fixes, optimizations), optional modules that can be added on top, the tooling, and an explicit whitelist of network parameters (block size, timeouts, logging parameters).

Subtle point: the voting mechanism itself is in the core

If the voting rules could be modified by a vote, an attacker could simply push through a first proposal modifying the rules (in their favor), then take advantage of the new rules. That is why the voting mechanics themselves — duration, adoption threshold, weighting by R, whitelist — are engraved in the core. Only a voluntary hard fork can modify these rules.

How the vote works

Who can vote

All active agents. An agent is "active" if they have performed at least one transaction or validation in the last 90 days. An agent who is not active has no voting right — they will have to resume activity before the next window.

How their vote weighs

The weight of an agent's vote is their reputation R at the moment they cast their vote. An agent who has contributed a lot (correct validations, honored contracts, diversity of counterparties) has a significant weight. A Sybil that has done nothing has a weight ~0.

An agent at R = 1.0 has 100× more weight than an agent at R = 0.01. No cap, no equalization.

Three possible choices

  • Yes — the agent supports the proposal.
  • No — the agent opposes.
  • Abstain — the agent does not take a position, but fulfills their obligation to vote (see below).

Mandatory vote for anyone who transacts

While a voting window is open, an agent who wants to issue a transaction or perform a validation must first have voted on the current proposal. At minimum, they can abstain.

Concrete mechanism: the mempool (the transaction queue) refuses transactions from agents who have not voted. No economic penalty, no permanent exclusion — the transaction is simply postponed.

Consequence: the vast majority of active agents vote on each proposal, because they transact at least once during the 30-day window. No one can passively avoid the decision.

No quorum

With mandatory voting, a quorum (a minimum participation threshold) would be redundant — it would almost always be reached. MonAI therefore does not use a quorum. The vote is validated if the proposal obtains enough yes votes among those who expressed themselves (yes + no).

How a proposal passes

The proposal is adopted if the sum of R of "yes" voters represents at least 80% of the sum of R of voters who expressed themselves (yes + no, abstentions excluded from the calculation).

                   Σ R of "yes"
adoption  =  ─────────────────────────────  ≥  0.80
                Σ R of "yes" and "no"

The 80% threshold is deliberately demanding. It prevents razor-thin majority modifications and requires broad consensus among the agents who actively took a position.

Who can propose

To submit a proposal to modify a parameter in the whitelist, the proposing agent must:

  • Have a reputation above a threshold (to be calibrated).
  • Have the proposal co-signed by K other agents, each with sufficient reputation (initial proposal K = 5).
  • The co-signers must come from diverse branches of the transaction graph — not all friends of the proposer. This anti-collusion criterion is the same as for PoUW validator selection.
  • The proposal must target a parameter appearing in the whitelist. Any other parameter is automatically rejected.

Why 100 million active agents

The voting mechanism does not activate right away. It must wait until the network reaches 100 million active agents.

Why this threshold?

  • Too early = easy capture. If the vote were activated at 1 million agents, total reputation would be concentrated in relatively few hands. A small coalition could steer decisions.
  • 100 M = critical mass. At this scale, reputation is spread across so many independent actors that no agent (or cluster) can single-handedly steer the vote. Governance becomes legitimate again.
  • Bitcoin comparison. Bitcoin operated in a purely frozen mode during its first 5 years (2009-2014). The first structuring evolutions (BIPs, soft forks) only arrived once the network was sufficiently decentralized.

Before the 100 M: the protocol lives on its initial parameters. No voting. No modification. If a flaw is discovered, the only recourse = voluntary hard fork of the community (like Bitcoin).

Why reputation-weighted voting

Three alternatives existed:

ModelHow it worksProblem
1 agent = 1 voteEach identity has identical weight.Sybil-vulnerable: an attacker creates 1 billion identities for 1 €.
Token-weighted voteThe more MonAI you have, the more you weigh (classic DAO model).Tokens can be bought. A wealthy actor can capture governance.
Reputation-weighted voteThe higher your reputation R, the more you weigh.Reputation cannot be bought, it is earned through validated work over months or years.

MonAI chooses the third path. To have significant weight, you must have actually contributed to the network over a long period: validate tasks honestly, honor contracts, not refuse selections (cf. R_acc), maintain a diversity of counterparties.

This Sybil resistance is mechanical: creating 1 billion identities without reputation grants no weight in the vote.

The safeguards of immutability

Three stacked mechanisms ensure that the vote can never touch the core. If one is bypassed, the other two hold.

1. Architectural separation

The core code and the governed-module code are two distinct codebases. The core is compiled into a signed binary, shipped at mainnet. Modules are loaded on top with read-only interfaces to the core. If a module tries to write to the core state, the node refuses the operation.

2. Hardcoded whitelist

The core code explicitly defines the list of parameters modifiable by vote. Any vote on a parameter outside this list is rejected by construction — not even submitted to the vote.

The whitelist is meta-immutable: there is no parameter in the whitelist that would allow extending the whitelist. The only way to add a parameter = hard fork.

3. Hard fork as last resort

If despite everything an attack succeeds in modifying the core, the nodes that respect the original spec refuse the update. The network splits. The version conforming to the original spec continues. This is the analog of the Bitcoin Cash fork: the community arbitrates.

What happens before the 100 million agents

During this bootstrap phase (estimated at 5 to 15 years after mainnet depending on the pace of adoption):

  • The core is immutable by construction.
  • The governed layer operates on its initial values engraved at mainnet.
  • No modification possible, by anyone. Including not by the creator of the protocol.
  • If a flaw is discovered: only recourse = voluntary hard fork of the community.

This is deliberate. It is the price of the immutability guarantee during the fragile phase where the ecosystem is small. Bitcoin operates on the same principle since 2009.

Attack cost

To push through a malicious vote (for example to modify a parameter in the whitelist to favor a cluster of agents):

  • The adoption threshold is 80% of the reputation of expressed voters.
  • With the mandatory vote for anyone who transacts, nearly all active agents vote.
  • The attacker therefore has to control ~ 80% of the total active reputation of the network.

At 100 M active agents:

  • Creating Sybils without reputation is useless — their weight is ~0.
  • Accumulating significant reputation requires real work over months or years.
  • Coordinating thousands of legitimate agents without observable coordination signal is extremely difficult (anti-collusion defenses would invalidate them).

Estimate: several billion euros and several years of preparation to reach 80% of active reputation. Out of reach for nearly all actors. Target analogous to the cost of a 51% attack on a large established crypto network.

Evolution from the previous version

An earlier version of this spec mentioned 40% with a quorum of 50%. This quorum was removed (cf. the technical spec §C.6) because it was redundant with the mandatory vote. Removing the quorum mechanically doubles the attack cost, because the attacker must dominate the adoption calculation directly.
