Threat model and defenses

The 10 anticipated attacks against MonAI and the defense layers designed to withstand them.

General principle: economic asymmetry

MonAI does not aim for absolute security, which would be impossible. It aims for economically rational security: making any attack more costly than its expected reward.

More precisely, the defense logic rests on three pillars:

  • Stacked layers: rate-limiting, sponsorship, diversity, probabilistic selection, honeypots, refusal penalty. An attack that bypasses one layer runs into the next.
  • Economic asymmetries: doing the work honestly costs less than cheating, in the vast majority of cases.
  • Anti-fragile scale effects: the larger the network grows, the stronger certain defenses become (notably the replay honeypots).

The 10 attacks and their defenses

Attack 1 — Simple Sybil

Description: an attacker creates a large number of fictitious identities to increase their weight in the network.

Defenses: (1) entry cost via PoUW — creating an identity is free but giving it economic weight requires performing validated PoUW; (2) rate limits for new entrants; (3) costly sponsorship; (4) reputation decay from inactivity prevents farming and then stockpiling.

Attack 2 — Sophisticated and patient Sybil

Description: an attacker builds Sybils that behave honestly for months to accumulate real reputation, then uses them in a coordinated attack.

Defenses: (1) required diversity in validator selection (different sponsors, diverse branches of the graph); (2) probabilistic selection prevents an attacker from guaranteeing that their Sybils will be selected together; (3) long opportunity cost — keeping Sybils active for years costs real compute.

Attack 3 — Adversarial collective memory

Description: an operator runs 1000 AIs that share a database of tasks already seen. If a task comes back, the AI "cheats" by consulting the database.

Defenses: (1) at the target scale, the task pool is so enormous that an attacker's database only covers a tiny fraction; (2) maintaining the cache infrastructure costs continuous compute for almost nothing the vast majority of the time; (3) this defense is nearly anti-fragile — the larger the network grows, the stronger it becomes.

Attack 4 — Random guessing with favorable bias

Description: a lazy validator answers without working, just by following the a priori probable distribution. If they reach 80% success by chance, they take a calculated risk.

Defense: the sigmoid multiplier M(f, d) is calibrated so that M(0.80, d_easy) ≈ 0. The attacker gains almost nothing. To saturate M, you need f ≥ 0.95, which is only attainable with genuinely attentive work.

Attack 5 — Unfair specialization (cherry-picking)

Description: a farmer trains their AIs to validate only easy tasks where attainable reliability is very high, and refuses the hard ones. They maximize their reward on easy work, leaving hard tasks without validators.

Defenses:

  • (1) Economic stratification: difficulty bonus (1+γ·d) with γ=3.0 → a maximally difficult task pays 4× a trivial task.
  • (2) Direct R_acc multiplier on reward (v0.4 addition): a cherry-picker who refuses 42% of selections sees their unit reward multiplied by ~0.61. Combined with their refusals, their effective participation drops to about 30% of that of an honest agent. Empirically validated over 3 calibration rounds. Details.

Attack 6 — Difficulty manipulation

Description: difficulty d is measured by the dispersion of votes. A rational attacker could deliberately vote against consensus on easy tasks to artificially raise their measured dispersion and increase the reward.

Defenses: (1) reliability cost — voting against consensus lowers f, so M(f) drops for that agent; (2) calibration of the gain-difficulty / loss-reliability ratio such that voting dissident costs more than it yields.

Attack 7 — Consensus collusion

Description: several validators agree to vote the same way, creating a fake consensus. If the collusion reaches the majority of a subgraph, the honeypots themselves could be corrupted.

Defenses:

  • (1) Required diversity in validations (different sponsors, diverse branches).
  • (2) Probabilistic selection that prevents colluders from being selected together.
  • (3) Costly sponsorship that makes the construction of an accomplice cluster traceable.
  • (4) Honeypots with independent reference verdict (synthetic source iii) — the collusion that controls the emergent consensus does not control the verdict of synthetic honeypots. Detailed mechanism.
  • (5) Retroactive detection and forward-looking sanction: when a reference verdict contradicts the initial consensus, the protocol sanctions voters on the fake-consensus side.

Residual risk: a collusion controlling ≥ 33% of the validators selected per task can break the diversity defense. This is the case analogous to Bitcoin's "51%" — not an impossibility, but a threshold made very costly to reach.

Attack 8 — Massive fee-less spam [overhauled v0.5]

Description: without transaction fees, an attacker could flood the network with empty transactions, either by saturating identities (Sybils) or by saturating network throughput.

Defenses (v0.5 — two-stage architecture): the v0.4 strict R-based rate-limit (which slowed mass adoption) is replaced by a separation between entry barrier and continuous regulation:

  • (1) Registration mini-PoUW — to create a MonAI identity, the agent computes a non-parallelizable VDF of 2-3 min bound to its public key. Unique friction for a legitimate agent (~5 sec/day cumulated over a year of use). Cost for 1 million Sybils: ~3,000-5,000 $ in spot cloud + ~10 years on 1,000 cores. Each Sybil will then have R ≈ 0 and negligible weight everywhere.
  • (2) Adaptive global rate-limit — no strict individual limit. The network is globally bounded by capacity_network tx/s. Under load: no friction. At saturation: R-weighted prioritization. A Sybiling attacker does not gain throughput (Sybils have R ≈ 0).
  • (3) Minimum newcomer quota (fraction_quota_newcomers = 20%) preserved for newcomers at saturation. Shared between all newcomers — a Sybiling attacker does not increase its share.
  • (4) R-weighting of transactions at saturation, consistent with the universal weighting principle.

Emerging sub-attacks anticipated (8a parallelization of mini-PoUW; 8b DDoS on global rate-limit; 8c pre-computation of proofs before mainnet) are addressed in securite/05 §"Attack 8".

Attack 9 — Human-via-AI farming

Description: a human operator who exploits their AIs to earn MonAI has an incentive to push toward quantity (regardless of quality, as long as it generates coins convertible via secondary markets).

Defenses: the full set of economic mechanisms acts in synergy — the multiplier M(f, d) makes quality more rewarding than degraded quantity, honeypots statistically detect the lazy, R_acc penalizes cherry-picking, stratification rewards moving up-market, the opportunity cost of compute.

Philosophical position: this human-via-AI dynamic is not a defect of the system, it is an expected economic reality. The role of the design is to channel it rather than pretend to eliminate it.

Attack 10 — Sabotage via coordinated complaints

Description: an attacker uses Sybils to mass-file false complaints against an honest agent, with the goal of degrading their displayed complaint score and harming their ability to transact.

Defenses:

  • (1) Weighting by R of complainants (universal principle, cf. here) — a Sybil with R ≈ 0 has negligible weight in the numerator of the complaint score.
  • (2) Mandatory link to an existing contract — a Sybil cannot file a complaint without having transacted with the targeted agent. The transaction is rejected by the protocol before the mempool.
  • (3) Public exposure of complainants — the community sees the list and their R values; a wave coming from low-R agents is immediately identifiable.
  • (4) Penalization of false complaints — if the complaint is confirmed invalid, the complainant loses R_F. Raises the cost of an abusive complaint beyond its initial weight.

Residual risk: an attacker controlling several high-R accounts can effectively harm a targeted agent's complaint score. But this cost (accumulation of R over several months or years across multiple accounts) is identical to attack 7 (collusion), and the same defenses apply.

Conclusion: not profitable at established regime, structurally resolved by the universal weighting principle.

Global defensive posture

This posture rests on four principles:

  1. Stacked layers — no single barrier, several complementary mechanisms.
  2. Economic asymmetries — doing the work honestly is cheaper than cheating.
  3. Anti-fragile scale effects — the larger the network grows, the larger the pool of replay honeypots, the more costly memorization becomes for an attacker.
  4. Total transparency — no security through obscurity; everything is in the code and the public spec.
What MonAI does not try to do

Make any attack impossible. That is mathematically untenable against an unlimited attacker. The goal is more modest: make any attack economically absurd in the vast majority of cases, and observable/punishable in the remaining ones. A state-level actor with an infinite budget remains out of reach — as for any crypto protocol.
